Common Specimens



Butterfly

Delicate winged insects with scaled wings, known for their remarkable metamorphosis from caterpillar to adult and their vital role in pollination. Butterflies are vibrant, flying insects known for their delicate, scaly wings, six legs, and two antennae. They undergo a fascinating metamorphosis from caterpillars to adults, feeding primarily on flower nectar. As crucial pollinators found worldwide, they represent environmental health and are renowned for long-distance migrations, such as the Monarch.


🦋 ORDER • LEPIDOPTERA

HoneyBee

Highly social insects that live in colonies of up to 60,000. They communicate through the famous "waggle dance" to share the location of flowers. They play a critical role in pollination, which supports plant biodiversity and agricultural crop yield, contributing to the sustainability of ecosystems. These highly organized insects live in colonies comprised of a single queen, thousands of female worker bees, and male drones.


🐝 ORDER • HYMENOPTERA

Silkworm

Fully domesticated for over 5,000 years, this moth larva spins a cocoon of continuous silk thread up to 900 metres long in a single strand. A silkworm is the larva or caterpillar of the domesticated silk moth, Bombyx mori, and is globally renowned for its role in sericulture, the production of silk. These voracious eaters feed exclusively on mulberry leaves for nearly a month, growing up to 10,000 times their birth weight before spinning a protective cocoon.


🐛 ORDER • LEPIDOPTERA

Cricket

Nocturnal insects famous for their chirping song, produced by rubbing their wings together — a behaviour called stridulation. Crickets are orthopteran insects closely related to grasshoppers, known primarily for the musical chirping sound (stridulation) produced by males rubbing their wings together to attract mates. These nocturnal insects are found worldwide, living in diverse habitats ranging from grassy fields and forests to dark caves and human basements


🦗 ORDER • ORTHOPTERA


Fun Facts

Dung beetles can pull over 1,000 times their own body weight.
Cockroaches can survive up to a week without their head — they die eventually from dehydration, not blood loss.
Bees communicate through dance — the “waggle dance” tells other bees the exact direction and distance to food.
Butterflies taste with their feet using sensors called chemoreceptors.
