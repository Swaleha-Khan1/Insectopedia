Colony & Hive

A honey bee colony is a highly organized social unit consisting of one queen, thousands of female worker bees, and several hundred male drones, totaling up to 80,000 individuals. They live together in a hive (a natural cavity or man-made box), where they raise brood and store food in beeswax combs. • The Honey Bee Colony (The Inhabitants) A colony is a "superorganism," where individual bees cannot survive alone. Queen (1): The only fertile female, responsible for laying up to 2,000 eggs per day. Worker Bees (Thousands): Sterile females that perform all labor, including cleaning, feeding larvae, producing wax, guarding, and foraging. Drones (Hundreds): Male bees whose sole purpose is to mate with a new queen. Brood: Eggs, larvae, and pupae developing into adult bees. • The Beehive (The Home) A hive is the physical structure where the colony resides. Components: Modern hives contain removable frames, while older types or wild nests are fixed-frame. Honeycomb: Beeswax structures constructed within the hive with hexagonal cells for honey storage, pollen storage, and rearing brood. Structure: Inside, the colony maintains a, consistent climate, cooling the nest during hot weather through water collection and ventilation. Difference: A colony is the living group of bees, while a hive is the structure they live in. Colony Life Communication: Bees communicate via pheromones and "dances" to locate food and maintain social order.



Life Cycle

Honey Bee Life Cycle A colony contains one queen, seasonal drone bees (males), and tens of thousands of female worker bees. Key stages and behaviors: 1. ggs & larvae — Eggs are laid in wax honeycomb cells. Drones develop from unfertilised eggs (haploid); females from fertilised eggs (diploid). Larvae are fed royal jelly, then honey and pollen — except one larva fed solely on royal jelly, which becomes the next queen. 2. Worker roles — Young workers ("nurse bees") clean the hive and feed larvae. As they age, they shift to building comb, receiving nectar, guarding, and eventually foraging outside. 3. The waggle dance — Workers communicate food source locations through a "dancing" pattern. Resources very close to the hive trigger a simpler "round dance." 4. Mating — Virgin queens fly out to mate with multiple drones from other colonies. The drones die after mating. 5. Swarming — New colonies form not through a lone queen but via *swarms* — a mated queen plus a large group of workers. They move to a pre-scouted nest site, build new comb, and raise a fresh brood.


Habitats

Honey bees are remarkably adaptable insects found across nearly every continent except Antarctica. Their habitat choices reflect both their biological needs and their highly organized social structure. Natural Habitats: In the wild, honey bees seek out sheltered, enclosed spaces that offer protection from weather and predators. Common nesting sites include hollow tree trunks, rock crevices, cliff faces, and underground cavities. Some species, like the giant honey bee, prefer building exposed open-air combs on large tree branches or cliff overhangs. Geographical Distribution: Honey bees are native to Europe, Africa, and Asia, but have been introduced to the Americas and Australia through human activity. They thrive in tropical and subtropical forests rich in flowering plants, temperate woodlands and meadows, arid scrublands, and even mountainous regions at high altitudes, having adapted to a surprisingly wide range of climates. What Makes a Good Habitat: A suitable habitat generally offers abundant flowering plants for nectar and pollen, a reliable water source, shelter from wind and rain, and open foraging land within a 2 to 3 km radius of the hive. Bees are highly selective about nesting locations and will scout extensively before committing to a site. Threats to Honey Bee Habitats: Honey bee habitats are increasingly under pressure from deforestation, urbanization, pesticide use, and climate change. These factors reduce nesting sites, limit food availability, and disrupt the seasonal flowering patterns that bees depend on. Protecting green spaces and planting wildflowers are among the simplest and most effective ways to support healthy bee habitats.
