01 — Behaviour

Diet & Sound

Crickets are omnivorous insects known for their distinct nocturnal chirping, produced by males to attract mates and establish territory. Their diet consists of a wide variety of plant and animal matter, while their sound production is heavily influenced by environmental temperature. As opportunistic omnivores, crickets consume both plant and animal materials. They feed on decaying plants, fungi, and forest debris; eat leaves, seeds, fruits, and nectar; and even prey on other insects — including pest species — helping to control their populations. In damp environments they also consume wet wood and organic matter. Male crickets produce sound through stridulation — rubbing a scraper on one wing against ridges, called a "file", on the other wing. The primary purpose is to attract female mates and repel rival males, with distinct quieter songs used for close-range courtship. Notably, chirping speed varies directly with temperature: crickets chirp faster in warmth and slow down in the cold, a relationship so reliable it has been used as an informal thermometer.
02 — Biology

Life Cycle

Crickets undergo incomplete metamorphosis, passing through three main stages — egg, nymph, and adult — with the full cycle usually spanning 2–3 months. Unlike butterflies or silkworms, there is no pupal stage; nymphs hatch looking like miniature adults and gradually develop into their final form through a series of moults. The egg stage begins when females use a sword-shaped organ called an ovipositor to deposit eggs into soil, sand, or decaying plant material. A single female can produce 200–1,500 eggs. Eggs typically hatch within 1–2 weeks, though many species overwinter as eggs and hatch in spring. Nymphs emerge wingless and lacking functional reproductive organs, growing and moulting 7–10 times over roughly 1–2 months. After each moult the cricket is initially bright white and soft, making it vulnerable until the new exoskeleton hardens and darkens. After the final moult, adults possess fully developed wings. They mate, and females begin laying eggs to restart the cycle. Most adult crickets live for only a few weeks, with many species dying in autumn as temperatures drop. Their brief adult lives are focused almost entirely on reproduction, ensuring the next generation is in place before winter arrives.
03 — Ecology

Habitat & Shelter

Crickets are found across the globe in warm, moist, and dark environments — from tropical rainforests and open grasslands to caves, beaches, and human homes. They are absent only from the most extreme cold regions. As primarily nocturnal insects, they spend the day sheltering in tight, dark spaces and emerge at night to feed and call. In natural settings, crickets hide under rocks, logs, leaf litter, or in self-dug burrows. They prefer high humidity and warmth, which is reflected in the exceptional diversity of species found in tropical regions. Meadows, fields, marshes, and forest floors all support healthy cricket populations, provided food and shelter are available nearby. Different species have adapted to distinct niches. House crickets frequently shelter indoors in basements and wall cavities, drawn to the warmth of chimneys and heating systems. Field crickets favour open pastures and tall grass. Mole crickets are highly specialised, living almost entirely within soil where they consume roots and organic matter using powerful, shovel-like forelegs. This ecological diversity makes crickets one of the most widespread and adaptable insect groups on Earth.
