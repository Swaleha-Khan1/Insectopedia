02 — Common Specimens

Notable Species

Butterfly
🦋 Order • Lepidoptera

Delicate winged insects with scaled wings, known for their remarkable metamorphosis from caterpillar to adult and their vital role in pollination. Butterflies are vibrant, flying insects known for their delicate, scaly wings, six legs, and two antennae. As crucial pollinators found worldwide, they represent environmental health and are renowned for long-distance migrations, such as the Monarch.

Honeybee
🐝 Order • Hymenoptera

Highly social insects that live in colonies of up to 60,000. They communicate through the famous "waggle dance" to share the location of flowers. They play a critical role in pollination, which supports plant biodiversity and agricultural crop yield. These highly organized insects live in colonies comprised of a single queen, thousands of female worker bees, and male drones.

View Entry →
Silkworm
🐛 Order • Lepidoptera

Fully domesticated for over 5,000 years, this moth larva spins a cocoon of continuous silk thread up to 900 metres long in a single strand. These voracious eaters feed exclusively on mulberry leaves for nearly a month, growing up to 10,000 times their birth weight before spinning a protective cocoon of lustrous, prized silk fibre.

View Entry →
Cricket
🦗 Order • Orthoptera

Nocturnal insects famous for their chirping song, produced by rubbing their wings together — a behaviour called stridulation. Closely related to grasshoppers, crickets are found worldwide in diverse habitats ranging from grassy fields and forests to dark caves and human basements. Their chirp rate famously correlates with ambient temperature.

03 — Curiosities

Fun Facts

🪲

Dung beetles can pull over 1,000 times their own body weight — pound for pound, the strongest animal on Earth.

🪳

Cockroaches can survive up to a week without their head. They die eventually from dehydration, not blood loss.

🐝

Bees communicate through dance — the "waggle dance" tells other bees the exact direction and distance to a food source.

🦋

Butterflies taste with their feet using sensors called chemoreceptors, landing on a leaf to instantly assess its suitability.
