Cocoon & Silk


Silk is a natural protein fiber produced by silkworms (most commonly Bombyx mori) to create a protective cocoon. Over 2–3 days, a single larva spins a continuous filament up to 1,000 meters long, consisting of fibroin (structure) and sericin (glue). Farmers harvest cocoons, killing the pupae inside to prevent breaking the fiber, then reel the silk. Key Aspects of Cocoons and Silk Production Production Process (Sericulture): Larvae feed on mulberry leaves, then spin a white-yellow cocoon. The Transformation: The larva transforms into a pupa inside the cocoon, providing a protected, warm environment for this phase. Reeling & Processing: Cocoons are placed in hot water/steam to soften the sericin (degumming) and allow the single, unbroken filament to be unraveled, or reeled. Harvesting Methods: Generally, pupae are killed during processing to keep the filament intact. However, in "Ahimsa" or peace silk, cocoons are used after the moth has naturally emerged. Composition: Silk is a strong natural fiber with a protein composition of 70-80% fibroin and 20-30% sericin. Uses: Beyond luxury textiles, silk is used in medical applications to stitch wounds and repair tendons due to its strength.



Life Cycle

The life cycle of a silkworm (Bombyx mori) consists of four main stages—egg, larva (silkworm), pupa (cocoon), and adult (moth)—typically completed in 45–55 days. Larvae hatch from eggs and feed on mulberry leaves, then spin a silk cocoon. The pupa transforms into a moth inside the cocoon. Key Stages in the Life Cycle: 1. Egg (10–14 days): A female moth lays up to 350-500 eggs, often on Mulberry leaves. These eggs remain dormant until hatching in warm conditions. 2. Larva/Silkworm (20–30 days): The hatched hairy caterpillars consume large quantities of Mulberry leaves. They grow rapidly and molt four times over roughly 27 days. 3. Cocoon (10–14 days): The larva spins a protective cocoon, made from a single, continuous silk thread that can be up to 900 meters long, using a protein secreted from its salivary glands. 4. Pupa: Inside the cocoon, the larva matures into a pupa, transforming from a yellow/white creature into a brown pupa. 5. Adult/Moth (3–4 days): The adult moth emerges from the cocoon using specialized enzymes, mates, and the female lays eggs, starting the cycle again. The moths are flightless and do not eat



Sericulture

Sericulture, or silk farming, is the agro-based industry of rearing silkworms—primarily Bombyx mori—for raw silk production, involving mulberry cultivation, caterpillar rearing, and reeling cocoons to extract silk fibers. It is a highly employment-oriented, labor-intensive, and eco-friendly rural enterprise, offering high returns on low investment. Types of Silk: The four main types of commercial silk produced are Mulberry, Eri, Tasar, and Muga. Major Producers: China is the world’s leading silk producer, followed by India. Economic Impact: It provides income for millions in rural India, with key production areas in Karnataka, Andhra Pradesh, Tamil Nadu, and Jammu & Kashmir. Life Cycle: Silkworms go through four stages: egg, larva (caterpillar), pupa (inside the cocoon), and moth, with the entire life cycle lasting about 50 days.
